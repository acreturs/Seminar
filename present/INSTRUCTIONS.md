# How to Present a Paper

## Structure & Timing (Total ~30 min)

### Define the problem (2 mins)
- What are the inputs and outputs?

### Motivation (3 mins)
- Why is this problem important?
- What are the issues of existing methods?

### Background / related works (5 mins)
- Don't spend a lot of time going paper one-by-one
- Cluster the papers and introduce them briefly

### Method (8 mins)
- What are the keys of their proposed method?
- Why is their proposed method effective (and better than previous methods)?

### Experiments (7 mins)
- How are the models trained?
- What are the datasets and evaluation metrics?
- What is the analysis of the results?
- What can we learn from the ablation study? Does it back up their claim?

### Discussion & limitations (5 mins)
- Your own critical analysis / demo

---

## Recommendations

- **Less text & more figures**
- **Add animation or show the information gradually**
  - Helps locate where you're at
  - Useful for big pipelines, slides with many bullet points
- **Practice with a timer** — 30 minutes goes fast
- **Use clear figures** — Re-draw key diagrams if it helps explanation; don't just screenshot from the paper

---

## Common Mistakes (Avoid!)

- Too much text
- Reading the slides
- Spending too long on background
- Presenting every experiment — focus on the ones that tell a story
